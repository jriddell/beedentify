Your match: Angelica

Common Names: Angelica or Wild Angelica Latin Name: Angelica sylvestris

Status: Native species to Scotland/UK

Basic Facts: A native across Europe, it is an annual or short lived perennial. It can grow up to 2.5m, which is why it can be confused with Giant Hogweed. It has similar purple-ish stems and small clusters of white flowers, in florets above the plant. Some plants also have slightly pink flowers. There are many garden cultivars that have been bred over the years, including a dark purple/ black variety.

Habitat: The last part of the latin name means it is most commonyl found in woodlands. It can also be found in fields, hedgerows, and marshy soils.

Fun Facts: It is in fact a non-native invasive in other countries that it has been taken to, notably Canada. Prior to 1900s it was commonly eaten as a vegetable, notably the stem eaten raw and the leaves stewed. Due to its nutritient content it was often taken as a cure for scurvy. It is also used as a traditional medicine and for dying cloth.

Your match: Butterbur

Common Names: Butterbur, Bog Rhubarb, Devil's Hat & Pestilence Wort Latin Name: Petasites japonicus

Status: Native to Scotland/UK

Basic Facts: A perennial plant that has different plants for the male and female of the species (called dioecious). The males are much more common than females. They flower in early spring, with clustered pink flowers on a central spike. The leaves can be large, the stems being around 1m in length and the leaves 1.5m in size. It is commonly confused with the invasive non-native Giant Butterbur, though this is typically present in North-East Scotland.

Habitat: It typically likes sloped or wet ground, and is typical along the banks of rivers.

Fun Facts: The root is being explored as a potential source of medicine for migraine treatment, and it has been used traditionally as a medicine for various aliments. The common name originates from the tendancy for dairy maids to wrap fresh patts of butter in the large leaves, in order to rest them.
